# Hidden Insights Guide

Purpose: a systematic technique catalog for surfacing what a meeting *meant* beneath what
was *said*. This is the differentiating layer of the analysis — run through every category
below before writing the hidden-insights section. Most meetings hide their most important
information in plain sight; these techniques make you look in the right places.

## 1. Listen for the unsaid

- **Vanishing agenda items** — topics announced at the start (or in the invite) that were
  never discussed, or were opened and quietly dropped. Something dropped under time
  pressure is often the thing someone preferred not to discuss.
- **Unanswered questions** — a question asked, followed by deflection, a joke, or a topic
  change. Log the question and who dodged it.
- **Missing stakeholders** — people or teams obviously affected by the decisions but never
  mentioned. Decisions made without them tend to unravel on contact.
- **Absent basics** — a project meeting with no mention of budget, deadline, or customer
  is telling you where the discomfort lives.

## 2. Detect tension and subtext

Linguistic tells that signal more than their literal content:

| Tell | Example | Likely subtext |
|---|---|---|
| Hedging | "I guess we could try that…" | Low commitment, expects failure |
| Over-qualification | "Just to play devil's advocate…" | Real objection, made safe to say |
| Sudden formality | Shift from casual to stiff phrasing | Discomfort or conflict entering |
| Deflecting humor | Joke exactly when stakes rise | Anxiety about the topic |
| Passive voice | "Mistakes were made", "it was decided" | Avoiding attribution or blame |
| Repetition | Same point restated 3+ times | Speaker feels unheard |
| Abrupt silence | Talkative person goes quiet after topic X | Disagreement or resignation |
| Over-agreement | "Totally, absolutely, 100%" then no follow-through commitment | Social yes, not a real yes |

## 3. Test agreements for fragility

For every decision or "yes", ask:

1. Does it have an **owner** who accepted it on the record?
2. Does it have a **date**?
3. Did the person with the power to deliver it agree — or only the person's boss?
4. Was any dissent voiced earlier and never resolved before the "agreement"?
5. Was it agreed in the last two minutes under time pressure?

Two or more red flags → label the agreement fragile and say why. Fragile agreements are
the single most common cause of "but we decided this last week!" meetings.

## 4. Find misalignments

- **Same word, different meanings** — two people say "MVP", "done", "soon", or "owner"
  and clearly mean different things. Quote both usages side by side.
- **Different exit states** — reconstruct what each key participant likely believes was
  decided. If the reconstructions differ, that gap is a headline finding.
- **Goal divergence** — one person optimizes for speed, another for quality, another for
  cost, and nobody named the trade-off. The conflict is deferred, not absent.

## 5. Read the power map

- Who actually made each decision vs. who formally holds the authority?
- Whose objections got engaged with, and whose got politely parked ("good point, let's
  take that offline")? "Offline" is where objections go to die — track them.
- Who did people look to (address, defer to) before committing?
- Did anyone's proposal get adopted after being ignored when a lower-status person said
  the same thing earlier? Classic signal of status-driven listening.

## 6. Name the unnamed risks

Scan for risk-shaped facts that were mentioned but never labeled as risks:

- Dependencies on a single person, vendor, or approval.
- Deadlines mentioned in passing that collide with commitments made elsewhere in the
  same meeting.
- Assumptions stated as facts ("the client will be fine with it", "legal won't have
  issues") with no verification plan.
- Resource math that doesn't close (scope grew, team didn't).

## 7. Calibrate before publishing

For every hidden insight, force the triple: **evidence → interpretation → confidence**.

- **High** — multiple independent signals point the same way (e.g., hedged agreement +
  no owner + earlier unresolved objection).
- **Medium** — one clear signal with a plausible alternative reading.
- **Low** — a single weak signal; include only if the stakes make it worth flagging.

Discard insights that fail two tests: *Would a neutral reader see the evidence the same
way?* and *Would the user do anything differently if this were true?* If it changes no
action and rests on thin evidence, it is noise — cut it. Five sharp insights beat fifteen
speculative ones.
