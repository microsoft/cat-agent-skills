# Persona Identification Framework

Purpose: profile meeting participants from behavioral evidence so the user understands
who they are dealing with — how each person communicates, what they want, and how much
weight they carry. A good persona profile lets the user predict how that person will act
in the *next* meeting.

## 1. Evidence sources

Build personas only from what the material shows:

| Signal | What it reveals |
|---|---|
| Talk time and turn-taking | Engagement, dominance, or disengagement |
| Interruptions (made / received) | Status and power dynamics |
| Questions vs. statements | Explorer vs. advocate posture |
| Vocabulary (numbers, feelings, process words) | Thinking style |
| Who they address / respond to | Alliances and reporting lines |
| What triggers their engagement | Their real stake in the topic |
| Hedging vs. assertive phrasing | Confidence and perceived safety |

## 2. Profile dimensions

For each participant, assess:

1. **Role and stake** — formal role if stated; otherwise infer from what they answer for.
   Ask: what does this person lose if the project fails?
2. **Communication style** — place on two axes:
   - Direct ↔ Indirect (says it plainly vs. wraps it in qualifiers)
   - Analytical ↔ Relational (leads with data vs. leads with people/impact)
3. **Position on the topics** — what they advocated, resisted, or stayed neutral on.
4. **Influence** — measured by outcomes, not volume: whose proposals survived? Who changed
   others' minds? Who was deferred to when they finally spoke?
5. **Emotional register** — enthusiastic, guarded, frustrated, checked-out. Note shifts
   during the meeting ("engaged until the budget came up, then silent").

## 3. Behavioral archetypes

Use these as shorthand, not boxes. A person can blend two, and archetypes can shift
between topics.

- **The Driver** — pushes for decisions and speed; allergic to open-ended discussion.
  Watch for: steamrolling unresolved concerns that resurface later.
- **The Skeptic** — probes, challenges, stress-tests. Valuable but can read as blocking.
  Watch for: whether their objections were answered or just outlasted.
- **The Diplomat** — smooths conflict, reframes, seeks consensus.
  Watch for: papering over real disagreement to end the meeting pleasantly.
- **The Expert** — speaks rarely but with technical authority.
  Watch for: warnings delivered once, softly, then dropped — often the most important
  sentence in the meeting.
- **The Sponsor** — senior voice framing strategy and unlocking resources.
  Watch for: what they choose *not* to react to; silence from a sponsor is a signal.
- **The Ghost** — present but nearly silent.
  Watch for: whether silence means agreement, disengagement, or dissent that felt unsafe
  to voice. Never assume agreement.
- **The Connector** — links topics to other people and teams ("we should loop in…").
  Watch for: the map of external stakeholders they reveal.

## 4. Rules of rigor

- Cite behavior, not vibes: "asked 6 of the 8 questions in the meeting" beats "seems curious".
- Distinguish *this meeting* from *this person*: one guarded meeting doesn't make someone
  a guarded person. Frame profiles as "in this meeting, X behaved as…".
- With unlabeled transcripts, only distinguish speakers when the text clearly supports it,
  and mark the profile as inferred.
- Personas are for insight, not judgment. Describe behavior neutrally; never mock or
  psychoanalyze beyond what the evidence supports.
