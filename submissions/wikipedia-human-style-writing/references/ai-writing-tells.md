# AI writing tells — field guide

Distilled from Wikipedia's [Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing),
reframed from *detecting* AI text into *avoiding* it while writing. These are
**signs, not sins**: any one can appear in good human writing. The problem is
their **density and mechanical repetition**. Treat this as a checklist to sweep a
draft against — and, crucially, fix the underlying vagueness, not just the surface
word.

---

## 1. Undue emphasis on significance, legacy, and broader trends

LLMs puff up importance by tying arbitrary details to some grand trend.

Watch phrases: *stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, plays a vital/crucial/
pivotal/key role, underscores/highlights its importance, reflects broader,
symbolizing its enduring/lasting, contributing to the, setting the stage for,
marking/shaping the, represents a shift, key turning point, evolving landscape,
focal point, indelible mark, deeply rooted.*

Also: manufactured "debates" ("has generated debate about…", "raises philosophical
questions about…"), and, for biology/geography, tacked-on ecosystem, conservation,
or cultural-heritage significance even when tenuous.

Fix: state the specific fact and stop. Delete the significance flourish unless a
source actually asserts it.

## 2. Canned emphasis on notability, attribution, and media coverage

LLMs "prove" importance by hammering that a subject was covered somewhere.

Watch phrases: *independent coverage, regional/national media outlets, trade
publications, profiled in, written by a leading expert, maintains an active social
media presence, garnered significant coverage.*

Fix: report the fact plainly; cite once if needed. Don't editorialize about how
notable the coverage makes the subject.

## 3. Superficial analysis (trailing "-ing" clauses)

Empty analysis bolted onto sentence ends, usually as a present participle.

Watch phrases: *highlighting…, underscoring…, emphasizing…, reflecting…,
symbolizing…, contributing to…, fostering…, ensuring…, enhancing…, cementing its
role as…, further solidifying…* — plus "valuable insights", "resonate with".

Example smell: "…the population stood at 56,998, **creating a lively community**
and **further enhancing its significance as a dynamic hub**."

Fix: cut the participle tail. If the analysis matters, state it as a plain
attributed claim, not a decorative flourish.

## 4. Promotional / advertisement-like tone

Even asked for neutrality, LLMs drift into travel-brochure or press-release voice.

Watch words: *boasts a, vibrant, rich, profound, nestled, in the heart of,
groundbreaking, renowned, breathtaking, seamless, showcasing, exemplifies,
commitment to, natural beauty, diverse array, unwavering, dynamic.*

Fix: neutral, concrete description. Remove adjectives that praise rather than
describe.

## 5. Vague attribution / overgeneralized opinion (weasel words)

Opinions pinned to a fog of unnamed authorities, or a couple of sources inflated
into a consensus.

Watch phrases: *industry reports, observers have noted, experts argue, some critics
argue, it is widely regarded, several sources/publications* (when only one or two
exist), *such as* before a suspiciously exhaustive list.

Fix: name the source, or drop the claim. Don't imply "many" when you have one.

## 6. Outline-like "Challenges / Future prospects" conclusions

A rigid formula: "Despite its [praise], [subject] faces challenges, including…"
ending in vague optimism or "future outlook" speculation.

Fix: only include challenges/future material that is specific and sourced. Avoid
the templated section that exists just to round the piece off.

## 7. High density of "AI vocabulary"

One such word is nothing; a cluster is the single strongest tell. They co-occur.

- **2023–mid-2024 (GPT-4 era):** additionally, boasts, bolstered, crucial, delve,
  emphasizing, enduring, garner, intricate/intricacies, interplay, key, landscape,
  meticulous, pivotal, underscore, tapestry, testament, valuable, vibrant.
- **Mid-2024–mid-2025 (GPT-4o era):** align with, bolstered, crucial, emphasizing,
  enhance, enduring, fostering, highlighting, pivotal, showcasing, underscore,
  vibrant.
- **Mid-2025 on (GPT-5 era):** emphasizing, enhance, highlighting, showcasing (plus
  the notability/coverage vocabulary above).
- **Grok-flavored:** causal, empirical, correlate, underscore.

Note: overuse of a word does **not** implicate its synonyms, and context matters
("underscore" can mean a literal underline). Target clusters, not isolated uses.

## 8. Avoidance of plain "is/are" copulas

LLMs swap simple *is/are/has* for inflated verbs.

Watch phrases: *serves as, stands as, functions as, operates as, represents,
marks, boasts, features, offers, maintains, refers to* (as a lead-in for a thing,
not a term).

Smell: "Gallery 825 **serves as** the exhibition space and **features** four
spaces" → "Gallery 825 **is** the exhibition space and **has** four spaces."

Fix: prefer the plain copula/verb unless the fancier verb adds real meaning.

## 9. Negative parallelisms

"Not just X, but Y" and "It's not A, it's B" constructions, and reversed variants.

Watch patterns: *not only… but also…, it's not just… it's…, no… no… just…, isn't
about X — it's about Y.*

Fix: state what the thing *is* directly. Reserve contrast for when you're genuinely
correcting a misconception with evidence.

## 10. Rule of three, overused

Reflexive triads: "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "phrase, phrase, and
phrase," used to make thin analysis feel thorough.

Fix: vary list length. Two items, or four, or a single precise word, when that's
what the content calls for. Don't manufacture a third element for rhythm.

## 11. Elegant variation / forced synonym-swapping

Over-avoiding repetition, so the same thing gets three different labels in a
paragraph ("the artist… the creator… the visionary").

Fix: it's fine to repeat the plain noun. Vary wording for meaning, not to dodge a
repetition penalty.

## 12. Formatting and punctuation tells

- **Title Case Headings** — capitalizing every main word in section headings. Use
  sentence case.
- **Overuse of boldface** — bolding every key term, "key takeaways" style, or
  `**Bold header:** description` list items. Bold sparingly, for genuine emphasis.
- **List item = bold lead-in + colon + description**, repeated mechanically. Prefer
  prose or lighter lists.
- **Rule-of-three bullet dumps** that restate the same shape over and over.
- **Em dash overuse** — em dashes (—), often space-surrounded, standing in for
  commas, colons, or parentheses in a punchy, sales-like cadence. Prefer varied,
  ordinary punctuation.
- **Emoji as formatting** — checkmarks, rockets, keys as bullets or section
  markers. Remove in prose.
- **Markdown artifacts** where they don't belong (stray `**`, `#`, `-`/`•` bullets
  pasted as literal characters).
- **Curly "smart quotes"** and other copy-paste-from-chatbot residue.
- **Uniform section-summary paragraphs** and "In conclusion / In summary / Overall"
  wrap-ups that add nothing.

## 13. Structural / content-level tells

- **Regression to the mean:** specifics blurred into generic praise ("inventor of
  the first train-coupling device" → "a revolutionary titan of industry"). Keep the
  specific, unusual, concrete fact.
- **Hedging preambles** that concede a subject is minor, then expound on its
  importance anyway.
- **Leads that define a descriptive title as a proper noun** ("*List of songs about
  Mexico* is a curated compilation that…").

---

## How to use this guide

1. Read the draft for meaning first; note the core facts and the author's stance.
2. Sweep for **clusters** of the tells above (density matters more than any single
   hit).
3. For each, fix the **underlying** issue — restore a specific fact, attribute a
   real source, cut empty significance — not just the surface word.
4. Preserve meaning, facts, figures, quotes, and the author's voice. Don't
   "de-AI" a draft into blandness or strip legitimate emphasis that the source
   supports.
5. Remember the guide is **descriptive, not prescriptive**: these words and shapes
   are not banned, they're overused. Judgment beats a find-and-replace.
